## Summary

The Palo Alto City Council held a special meeting on February 23, 2026, addressing several key community priorities. Significant progress was reported on enhancing rail safety, including the implementation of 24/7 security at crossings and advanced anti-intrusion technology. The Council also received an update on the Oversized Vehicles (OSV) Ad Hoc Committee's ongoing efforts to balance compassion for individuals living in vehicles with neighborhood livability and public safety. Additionally, discussions covered city energy policies, potential state legislation impacts, and support for youth mental health services.

Key actions and discussion items included:

*   Introduction of live translation services for City Council meetings to promote inclusive participation.
*   Implementation of 24/7 security coverage at all four Palo Alto rail crossings and installation of anti-intrusion technology.
*   Initiation of a 60-day analysis for a potential temporary closure of the Churchill Avenue rail crossing.
*   The Oversized Vehicles Ad Hoc Committee reported on street cleaning, vehicle towing, and connecting individuals living in vehicles with services.
*   Discussion of a proposal from the Santa Clara County Board of Supervisors to fund the allcove Center for youth mental health.
*   Updates on state legislation that could save the City $1M-$2M annually in energy costs and efforts to rebuild electric transmission infrastructure.
*   Co-sponsorship of a symposium on sustainable data centers with Stanford University.

## Agenda Highlights

**Call to Order:** Mayor Veenker called the meeting to order. The City Clerk announced the implementation of free live translation services for Council meetings, available via the Wordly app, to enhance inclusive participation.

**Closed Session (Potential Litigation):** The Council met privately to discuss potential litigation regarding 156 North California Avenue. No reportable action was taken after returning from the closed session.

**Agenda Changes, Additions and Deletions:** The City Manager noted a late report concerning a contract with Orion Security for "Track Watch" services at Caltrain rail crossings.

**Public Comment:** Residents raised concerns about health risks associated with natural gas stoves and urged the Utilities Department to inform residents and promote electric stove adoption. Another resident expressed concerns about potential conflicts with open meeting laws due to overlapping Council committee memberships.

**Council Member Questions, Comments and Announcements:**
*   **Rail Safety:** Councilmembers reported significant progress on rail safety, including 24/7 security at all four rail crossings (with a contract for Orion Security pending approval) and the installation of anti-intrusion and behavioral detection technology. A 60-day analysis has begun for a potential temporary closure of the Churchill Avenue rail crossing.
*   **allcove Center:** An update was provided on a Santa Clara County Board of Supervisors proposal to fund the allcove Center for youth mental health, with the City potentially discussing its support later.
*   **Energy Updates:** Mayor Veenker shared updates on state legislation supporting publicly owned hydropower, which could save the City $1M-$2M annually after 2030. Discussions are ongoing regarding data center energy impacts and legislation to rebuild electric transmission infrastructure after wildfires. Staffing issues at the federal Central Valley hydropower project, a key energy source for Palo Alto, are being addressed.
*   **NCPA & Sustainable Data Centers:** The Northern California Power Agency (NCPA) is proceeding with a general manager search and evaluating a new membership application. The Mayor announced plans for a cross-sector symposium on sustainable data centers, co-sponsored with Stanford University, to discuss sustainability and policy.

**Study Session (Oversized Vehicles (OSV) Ad Hoc Committee):** The committee presented a status report on its efforts to address homelessness and neighborhood impacts related to oversized vehicles. Since October 2025, actions have included deep cleaning 17 street segments, adding biweekly street sweeping, and towing 33 non-compliant oversized vehicles after outreach. Case managers had 349 interactions with individuals living in vehicles to connect them with services. Community feedback gathered at an open house showed diverse views, including calls for stronger enforcement, support for safe parking, and requests for faster action. The committee will continue its work, incorporating Council and public feedback.
