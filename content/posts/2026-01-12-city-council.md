## Summary

The Palo Alto City Council held a special meeting on January 12, 2026, where they authorized the City Attorney to initiate litigation in one case after a closed session. The meeting also featured extensive public comment on topics ranging from city-wide electrification and the health impacts of gas stoves to concerns about the "3rdThursday" event and the city's fiber project. Council members made various announcements, and a significant portion of the meeting was dedicated to reviewing the progress on the City Council's 2025 priorities and objectives.

Key actions and discussions included:
*   **Authorized Litigation:** City Council approved the City Attorney to start legal action in one case.
*   **Public Comment on Electrification:** Residents urged the city to pursue an all-electric future, highlighting health risks associated with natural gas appliances.
*   **Public Comment on 3rdThursday Event:** Several community members expressed confusion and concern regarding the cancellation and leadership changes of the "3rdThursday" event on California Avenue.
*   **Review of 2025 Council Priorities:** Council discussed the progress on its four main priorities: Climate Action, Economic Development, Housing Strategies, and Public Safety, Wellness, and Belonging.

## Agenda Highlights

**Closed Session**
The Council met in a closed session to discuss potential litigation. Upon returning, Mayor Veenker announced that the City Council had authorized the City Attorney to initiate legal action in one specific case where the city would be the plaintiff.

**Public Comment**
Several residents spoke on a variety of issues. A recurring theme was the push for an all-electric Palo Alto, with multiple speakers emphasizing the health hazards of natural gas stoves, including emissions of benzene, methane, and nitrogen dioxide, and their link to respiratory issues like asthma in children. There was also significant discussion about the "3rdThursday" event on California Avenue, with residents expressing dismay over its cancellation, confusion about funding, and support for its former organizer. Other comments included a request for the city to extend its Dark Fiber network and concerns about a flyer for a City-sponsored Martin Luther King Jr. Day event.

**Council Member Questions, Comments and Announcements**
Mayor Veenker offered condolences regarding a recent shooting in Minneapolis and reiterated Palo Alto's policy of police independence from ICE, noting the city's involvement in lawsuits to protect federal funding. Council Member Lythcott-Haims also commented on the Minneapolis incident, mentioned support for those affected by youth suicide, and provided an update on the Cubberley ad hoc committee's work towards a fall ballot measure.

**Study Session: Review and Discuss 2025 Q4 Council Priorities & Objectives**
The Council reviewed the progress made on its four key priorities for 2025:
*   **Climate Action and Adaptation, & Natural Environment Protection:** 74% of objectives were completed in 2025.
*   **Economic Development and Retail Vibrancy:** This priority aims to improve Palo Alto's business climate. Updates included a 13% vacancy rate on University Avenue and a 6.4% rate on California Avenue (down from 12% a year prior). Work on downtown parking, cleanliness, and parklet options was highlighted.
*   **Implementing Housing Strategies for Social and Economic Balance:** Approximately 70% of objectives were completed or on track, with new ordinances introduced to increase housing production in commercial and multi-family areas.
*   **Public Safety, Wellness, and Belonging:** (Details not fully provided in the excerpt, but it was listed as a priority).

Overall, 66% of the 70 objectives supporting these four priorities were completed by the end of 2025, with 23% behind schedule, 7% on track with scope changes, and 4% on hold. The session also touched on the format for the upcoming 2026 City Council Annual Retreat.
